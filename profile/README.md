# How to Avoid Problems in Your Marriage (How to Overcome Challenges in Marriage)

Every couple starts out convinced they're different. You look at your partner across the table on your wedding day and think, we won't end up like them, the ones who stopped talking, who sleep in separate rooms, who stay together out of habit instead of love. 

I often hear from couples who tell me the same thing: nobody warned them how much work this would take. Not the wedding, not the honeymoon, the actual day-to-day work of staying close to another person for decades. Marriage doesn't fall apart in one dramatic moment, usually. It wears down slowly, through small neglects that pile up until two people who once knew everything about each other become strangers sharing a mortgage. The good news is that most marriage problems are predictable. And if something is predictable, you can prepare for it.

**Talk About The Small Stuff Before It Becomes Big Stuff**

Here's something I've noticed after years of sitting with couples in my office: the fights about dishes are never really about dishes. They're about feeling unseen. They're about one person carrying more weight than the other and not saying anything until it turns into resentment. When was the last time you told your partner something bothered you the moment it happened, instead of letting it sit?

Most couples wait too long to speak up. They tell themselves it's not worth the argument, so they swallow it. Then it happens again, and again, and one day it comes out sideways, way bigger than the original issue ever was. If something bothers you, say it while it's still small. A short, calm conversation today beats a blowup six months from now. This takes practice, especially if you grew up in a house where conflict meant yelling or silence. But marriages that last are usually built by two people who got comfortable saying uncomfortable things early, before those things had time to grow teeth.

**Protect Your Time Together Like It Matters, Because It Does**

Life gets full. Kids, jobs, bills, aging parents, a hundred small demands pulling at you from every direction. Somewhere in the middle of all that, couples stop making time for each other, not because they stopped caring, but because everything else felt more urgent. Ask yourself honestly: when did you last have a real conversation with your spouse that wasn't about logistics?

You cannot run on fumes and expect closeness to survive. Put time together on the calendar the same way you'd schedule anything else important. It doesn't need to be fancy. A walk after dinner, twenty minutes with the phones in another room, a drive with no destination. What matters is that it's consistent and it's just the two of you. Couples who protect this time, even in small doses, stay connected through hard seasons. Couples who let it slide often wake up years later feeling like roommates.

**Learn How Your Partner Actually Wants To Be Loved**

You'd be surprised how many arguments come down to this one thing: both people are trying, but neither one is landing. One partner shows love by fixing problems and taking care of practical needs. The other wants words, wants to hear it out loud. Neither is wrong, they're just speaking different languages, and nobody's translating.

Think about how you show love naturally. Now think about whether that's actually what your partner needs. It might not be. Ask them directly what makes them feel cared for, then believe the answer even if it's not what comes naturally to you. This isn't about becoming someone else. It's about stretching a little, on purpose, for the person you chose. Small gestures done consistently mean more than grand ones done rarely. Bring them coffee the way they like it. Notice when they're tired and take something off their plate without being asked. These little acts build a kind of trust that arguments can't easily break down.

**Fight Fair, Because You Will Fight**

Every couple fights. Anyone who tells you otherwise is either lying or hasn't been married long enough. The problem isn't the fighting itself, it's how you do it. Do you attack the person or the problem? Do you bring up old wounds that have nothing to do with tonight's argument? Do you walk away and shut down, leaving things unresolved for days?

Set some ground rules for yourselves before the next fight happens, because it will. No name calling. No bringing up things from three years ago. No walking out without saying when you'll come back to the conversation. Stick to the actual issue in front of you. And here's something worth remembering: you're on the same team. The goal isn't to win the argument and watch your partner lose. The goal is for both of you to understand each other better than you did before the fight started. If you keep that goal in mind, even hard conversations stay productive instead of destructive.

**Keep Growing As Individuals, Not Just As A Couple**

This one surprises people. You'd think spending every moment together would strengthen a marriage, but it often does the opposite. Marriages need two whole people, not two halves clinging to each other for identity. Have you kept up with the things that made you interesting before you got married? The hobbies, the friendships, the goals that were yours alone?

When you lose yourself entirely inside a marriage, you have less to bring back into it. You become dependent instead of connected, and those are not the same thing. Keep learning things. Keep your own friendships. Keep chasing goals that have nothing to do with your spouse. A marriage made up of two people who are still growing individually stays interesting for decades. A marriage where both people gave up everything for the relationship often runs dry, because there's nothing new being brought back to share.

**Choose Each Other Again And Again**

Falling in love is easy. It happens without effort, without decision, almost like weather. Staying in love is a different matter entirely. It's a choice you make on ordinary Tuesdays when nothing is wrong and nothing is exciting either. Have you noticed how easy it is to take someone for granted once the excitement fades and daily life sets in?

This is the quiet secret behind marriages that last: the couples keep choosing each other, on purpose, even when the feeling isn't loud that day. They say thank you for the small things. They remember why they picked this person in the first place, even during the boring stretches, even during the hard ones. Love that lasts isn't the kind that never fades. It's the kind that gets renewed, again and again, by two people who decided it was worth the effort.

Saving a marriage isn’t about pretending the pain never happened. It’s about deciding that your relationship is worth fighting for, even when things feel distant, broken, or uncertain.

Right now, you may feel exhausted from the arguments, the silence, or the growing gap between you and your spouse. But the truth is, many couples who once felt hopeless were able to reconnect, rebuild trust, and fall in love again when they finally discovered the right guidance and took intentional action.

Your marriage story does not have to end here.

No matter how disconnected things may feel today, healing is possible when both love and effort still exist beneath the hurt. Small changes can open the door to deeper conversations, renewed intimacy, and the kind of connection you thought was lost forever.
